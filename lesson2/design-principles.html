Visual Hierarchy

CXL by Rohan Ayyar

Essentially, this is a way of page layout that visually conveys the order and importance of content. The visual hierarchy is created by drawing certain cues (such as size, color, contrast, shape, position, and arrangement) to create a sense of depth. We experience visual levels every day.

In order to provide you with a simple daily example, please consider what is most likely to jump out when you scroll through Samsung’s black, gray, and white interfaces? Of course a bold brand!

Although visual hierarchy is a key principle of website and product design, the concept itself is by no means difficult to grasp. However, handling it correctly will most likely require trial and error.

Hick's Law

Smashing Magazine by Jason Gross

Hick’s Law predicts that the time and effort required to make a choice will increase as the number of choices increases.

When response time is important, reduce the number of options. One to five is a good rule of thumb. Don’t you want to use all these buttons? Too many options with an equally perceived hierarchy can lead to analysis paralysis. This often leads to frustration. Not the best user experience.

On the contrary, systems with fewer options and clearer options are often rated by users as having a better user experience.

Fitt's Law

WebFX By William Craig

Fitts' law states that the amount of time a person needs to move a pointer (such as a mouse cursor) to the target area is a function of the distance to the target divided by the size of the target. Therefore, the longer the distance, the smaller the size of the target and the longer it takes.

This law is widely used in user experience (UX) and user interface (UI) design. For example, the law affects the practice of making interactive buttons larger (especially on finger-operated mobile devices)-clicking smaller buttons is more difficult (and time consuming). Likewise, the distance between the user's task/attention area and the button related to the task should be kept as short as possible.
